The Golden Rule of Pediatrics

Kids are NOT little adults. They compensate longer, crash faster. A "tired-looking" or "calm" child in distress = IMPENDING FAILURE. Act now.

Red Flags in Pediatrics = CRITICAL

🚨 Stridor (croup, epiglottitis, foreign body)
🚨 Drooling with fever (epiglottitis risk)
🚨 Retractions + nasal flaring (respiratory distress)
🚨 Grunting (respiratory effort, pain)
🚨 Cyanosis (central = bad, peripheral = less urgent)
🚨 Altered mental status (sepsis, head injury, intoxication)
🚨 Petechial rash + fever (meningococcemia = EMERGENCY)
🚨 "Tired" or "calm" look during distress = DECOMPENSATION

CPR Compressions & Ventilations (Pedi)

Compression Rate: 100–120/min (same as adults, all ages)
Compression Depth: At least 2 inches (5 cm) for 1–8 yr; ≥2 inches for >8 yr
Hand Position: 1-2 hands (infant = 2-finger); 2 hands (child)
Ventilation: 10–12/min, ~500 mL per breath (not huge puffs)

Airway by Age

Infant (0–12 mo): Head neutral (not extended). Use oral airway if unresponsive + no gag.
Toddler–School (1–12 yr): Sniffing position. OPA if unconscious + no gag. BVM if RR inadequate.
Adolescent (12+ yr): Treat like adult. Sniffing position. OPA/NPA per protocol.
KEY: Infants are obligate nose-breathers. Clear nasal passages first if distressed.

Shock in Kids (Pediatric Shock)

Early Shock (Compensated): Tachycardia, pale, cool extremities, normal BP (still compensating)
Late Shock (Decompensated): Bradycardia (bad sign!), hypotension, altered LOC, barely perfusing
KEY SIGN: Weak femoral pulse = shock. Compare to carotid. If weak, treat shock now.
Treatment: High-flow O₂, position supine, IV/IO fluids (ALS), rapid transport. DO NOT delay.

Croup (Laryngotracheobronchitis)

Age: 6 months – 3 years (classic)
Signs: Barky, seal-like cough + stridor (worse at night)
Fever: Low-grade typical
Treatment: Cool mist, cool air, supportive. O₂ if hypoxic. Most resolve on their own.
Red Flag: Severe stridor at rest, drooling, tripoding = possible epiglottitis (different, more serious)

Epiglottitis

Age: Often 2–6 years but any age
Classic Triad: Fever + sore throat + drooling
Position: Tripoding, leaning forward ("hot potato" voice)
DO NOT examine throat, do NOT agitate, do NOT lay flat. Keep parent with child.
Action: High-flow O₂, upright position, allow position of comfort, prepare for airway loss, RAPID transport

Foreign Body Aspiration (FBA)

Age Peak: 6 months – 4 years
Signs: Acute choking/coughing, stridor, unilateral decreased breath sounds (if lodged)
Treatment: High-flow O₂, position of comfort, rapid transport (let ALS/ENT remove)
DO NOT blind finger sweep. DO NOT induce vomiting. Careful suction if pooled secretions.

Febrile Seizure

Age: 6 months – 6 years (peak 18–24 mo)
Trigger: Rapid rise in fever (not necessarily high fever)
Type: Usually generalized tonic-clonic, <5 min, full recovery
Treatment: Protect from injury, high-flow O₂, rapid transport. Do NOT hold down. Position head to side.
NOT status epilepticus (unless recurrent without full recovery).

Dehydration Assessment (Mild → Severe)

MILD (3–5%): Normal BP, slightly dry mucous membranes, normal behavior
MODERATE (6–9%): Slightly decreased BP, dry mucous membranes, weak cry, delayed cap refill ≥2 sec
SEVERE (≥10%): Hypotensive, very dry mucous membranes, weak cry, delayed cap refill >2 sec, altered LOC
Action: Oral rehydration (mild), IV fluids (ALS, moderate–severe), rapid transport if severe

Abuse/Neglect Red Flags

🚨 Injuries inconsistent with age/story (infant with femur fracture? toddler with internal bleeding?)
🚨 Injuries in multiple stages of healing
🚨 Bite marks, cigarette burns, immersion burns (lines)
🚨 Subdural hematoma, retinal hemorrhages (shaken baby)
🚨 Neglect signs (severe malnutrition, poor hygiene, developmental delay)
ACTION: Document objectively. Report to authorities per protocol. Do NOT accuse parents.

Choking (Heimlich vs. Back Blows)

Infant (<1 yr): Back blows (5x) + chest thrusts (5x). Repeat until object out or unresponsive.
Child (>1 yr): Abdominal thrusts (Heimlich) above navel, below xiphoid. Repeat until object out.
Unconscious: Begin CPR. Check mouth between compressions, remove object if visible.

SIDS Risk Factors & Prevention

Risk Factors: Prone position, soft objects in crib, overheating, co-sleeping, prematurity, low birth weight
Prevention: Back sleeping, firm surface, room-sharing without bed-sharing, pacifier, avoid overheating
During Transport: Handle gently, maintain warmth, position supine with airway clear

👧 Pediatric Medications & Dosing

Weight-Based Dosing Quick Reference

Know the child's weight! Most pedi drugs are dosed by kg. Always ask caregivers. If unknown, use age-based estimates:

• Infant (1 mo): ~4 kg
• Infant (6 mo): ~7 kg
• Toddler (1 yr): ~10 kg
• Toddler (2 yr): ~12 kg
• Preschool (3 yr): ~15 kg
• Preschool (4 yr): ~16 kg
• School (6 yr): ~20 kg
• School (8 yr): ~25 kg
• Older child (10 yr): ~30–35 kg

EMT-B Medications (Pedi Scope)

Oxygen (O₂)
Dose: 10–15 L/min non-rebreather (if tolerates)
Infants/toddlers: May need high-flow mask or blow-by in fearful child
Oral Glucose
Dose: 5–10 g (depends on product, younger = less)
Use: Hypoglycemia, alert, can swallow, no trauma/seizure
Note: Older children = 15 g (standard)
Epinephrine Auto-Injector (Anaphylaxis)
Dose: 0.15 mg IM (<30 kg / <66 lbs)
Dose: 0.3 mg IM (≥30 kg / ≥66 lbs)
Route: Thigh IM. Massage 10 sec. Repeat every 5–15 min if needed.
Activated Charcoal (Ingestion)
Dose: 10–25 g (younger) to 50 g (older children)
Use: Alert, gag reflex, recent ingestion (per poison control)
Aspirin (if protocol allows)
Dose: Age-based or 10–15 mg/kg
Use: Chest pain, alert, no allergy
Caution: Avoid in viral illness (Reye syndrome risk)

ALS Medications (Know for Test)

Epinephrine IV (Cardiac Arrest)
Dose: 0.01 mg/kg IV/IO (typically 0.1 mL of 1:10,000)
Repeat every 3–5 min
Amiodarone (VF/VT)
Dose: 5 mg/kg IV/IO (max 300 mg first dose, 150 mg second)
Atropine (Bradycardia)
Dose: 0.02 mg/kg IV/IO (min 0.1 mg, max 0.5 mg single dose)
Note: Second dose = 0.04 mg/kg (max 1 mg)
Sodium Bicarbonate (Metabolic Acidosis)
Dose: 1 mEq/kg IV/IO (0.5–1 ampule for infant, 1–2 for child)
Naloxone (Opioid OD)
Dose: 0.1 mg/kg IV/IO/IM (min 0.4 mg, max 2 mg)
Note: Repeat every 2–3 min if no response
Dextrose (Hypoglycemia IV)
Dose: 0.5–1 g/kg IV (D25 or D50 depending on concentration)
Note: Infant-friendly D25 to avoid hyperosmolarity

Intraosseous (IO) Access Basics

When to use IO: Cardiac arrest, severe shock, unable to obtain IV
Sites (by age):
• <3 yr: Proximal tibia (below knee, medial)
• >3 yr: Proximal humerus (also acceptable)
Any medication/fluid can go IO = as fast as IV.

Pedi BVM Tips

Mask Size Matters:
• Too big = leaks, poor seal
• Too small = doesn't cover nose + mouth
Ventilation Volume: Use just enough to see chest rise (avoid over-inflation = gastric distention)
Rate: 10–12/min (same as adults)
